Mine Is Better

Than Yours!

Read why you think that yours is better than anybody else’s. It’s your brain, not you. And your ego.

 

The things that we own matter to us. Possessions become important and we attach more value to them than to objects that we don't own. The effect in which individuals evaluate objects that they possess or imagine to possess is known as the mere ownership effect or the endowment effect. Essentially, this effect suggests that simply having something makes us see it as being better and having more positive attributes than another object that we do not have. This applies to things that have emotional value and those that do not. What is more, it also applies to symbols and other non-material ideas or concepts. If we make it ours, we will think of it as being better.

 

The explanation behind this idea is related to associations. When we have ownership of an object or claim an idea as ours, we make an association between ourselves and the object or idea. The object is related to us, so the view that exists of this object has an effect on how we view ourselves on some level.

 

Generally speaking, we want to have a positive view of ourselves, so that makes us enhance the view we have of those objects. In short, our motivation to view ourselves in a positive light also makes us, unconsciously, view everything we associate with ourselves, including things we own, as being positive or better than other things.

 

It's easy enough to find how this effect affects behavior and perception, however, an interesting question is how does the mere ownership effect appears in the functioning of the brain and what implications this has.

 

Researchers from the Yale University conducted a study on the mere ownership effect to identify the neurobiological effects that it has.

There were two conditions in the study – one with a threat to the participants' self-esteem and one without this threat. In the absence of a threat to self-esteem, the brain regions that were activated were those that processed personal and affective significance, such as the ventromedial prefrontal cortex, the ventral anterior cingulate cortex and medial orbitofrontal cortex.

In the condition where there was a threat to self-esteem, different regions became implicated in the mere ownership effect. The regions that were activated were those that had to do with cognitive control processes.

These findings suggest that the mere ownership effect can appear in two different ways depending on whether there is or is not a threat to self-esteem. These conditions might seem artificial, but refer to different motivational situations. In one case, where there is no threat to a person's self-esteem, the person will still evaluate more positively the thing that they own. However, if there is a threat, more processes are engaged and different areas of the brain become active.

 

How might this situation look in practice? Let's say an individual is asked to compare two cars – the model that they have and the model that they don't own.

 

The individual will most likely, automatically and directly, rate their own model as having a better quality and being the better choice overall. In general, the person might only express a stronger preference for the car that they already own.

If a person is asked to choose between the two models, but with an added suggestion that the car that the person already owns is poor and an unsensible choice, it's likely that the person will perceieve this as a threat to their self-esteem and respond to the situation differently. A person might engage on a more cognitive level or react with a stronger desire to defend their choice.

 

The mere ownership effect, in short, can manifest through two very different neurobiological processes depending on whether or not there is a threat to the self-esteem of the person who is experiencing the mere ownership effect.
